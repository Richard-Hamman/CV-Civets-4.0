The Networking Myth: How to Actually Build Connections That Lead to Jobs.

Networking is often heralded as the key to career success, but for many, it feels like an elusive concept. Advice like "attend events," "hand out business cards," and "build your LinkedIn connections" can make networking seem performative or even superficial. The truth? Successful networking isn’t about the number of contacts you accumulate; it’s about building genuine, meaningful relationships that can open doors.

Here’s how to approach networking in a way that truly works:

  1. Shift Your Mindset: Give Before You Receive

    The best networks are built on mutual value, not transactions. Instead of approaching someone with a direct request for help, think about how you can offer something meaningful to them. This could be sharing an article they might find interesting, providing insights into a topic you’re knowledgeable about, or simply offering genuine support for their work.

    People are more likely to help when they feel you’ve invested in the relationship. Remember, authentic networking is a two-way street.

  2. Focus on Quality, Not Quantity

    It’s tempting to think that success lies in knowing as many people as possible. But if your connections are shallow, they’re unlikely to lead to meaningful opportunities. Aim to build a smaller circle of well-nurtured relationships.

    When you reach out, tailor your interactions. Reference shared interests or experiences to create a bond that feels personal and thoughtful.

  3. Leverage Shared Spaces

    Some of the best networking happens naturally in shared spaces, such as industry conferences, professional organizations, or online communities. Engage with people in these settings by contributing to conversations and demonstrating your expertise.

    On platforms like LinkedIn, participate in group discussions or comment on posts from industry leaders. These interactions can lead to meaningful connections that grow over time.

  4. Ask for Insights, Not Jobs

    A common networking mistake is directly asking for a job, which can feel transactional. Instead, focus on learning. Ask for advice, insights into the industry, or their personal experiences. People enjoy sharing their knowledge, and this approach often builds rapport and trust.

    Once you’ve established a connection, they’re more likely to think of you when opportunities arise.

  5. Stay Consistent and Follow Up

    Networking isn’t a one-and-done activity. Regularly follow up with your contacts to maintain the relationship. Share updates on your career, congratulate them on milestones, or simply check in to see how they’re doing.

    Even a quick message every few months can keep the connection alive and remind them of your presence in a natural way.

  6. Build Your Personal Brand

    Your online presence can be a networking tool in itself. Ensure your LinkedIn profile and other professional platforms reflect your skills and achievements. Post insights, share articles, or write about industry trends to showcase your expertise.

    A strong personal brand can attract like-minded professionals, making networking easier and more effective.

    So, how does one fix this? Build Connections That Matter

    Networking is less about schmoozing and more about building trust and mutual respect. By shifting your focus to giving value, fostering genuine relationships, and maintaining consistent communication, you’ll develop a network that doesn’t just exist but actively supports your career growth.

    In the end, meaningful connections—not just names on a list—are what open doors to exciting opportunities.
